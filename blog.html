Magnetic field measurement unit

The unit of measurement of magnetic induction is Gauss, symbol G (sometimes Gs), is also known as the magnetic flux density. One gauss is defined as one maxwell per square centimeter. The unit is part of the Gaussian system of units, which inherited it from the older CGS-EMU system. It was named after to the German mathematician and physicist Carl Friendrich Gauss in 1936. There is another unit for measuring the magnetic field. It's a Tesla and it's 10000G. Unfortunately, it has been scientifically proven that the magnetic field weakens over time. This phenomenon is indirectly reflected on every living organism on the planet. Only 500 years ago, the strength of the magnetic field in our country was 1 Gauss per cm2 and now it is half as much.

The strength of the earth's magnetic field

The Earth's magnetic field is constantly changing, and so is the way it changes. When describing the Earth's magnetic field, we must determine both the direction and intensity of the field. Since both change and change differently in different places, it is not easy to say how it changes the earth's field as a whole. Due to the complexity of the Earth's magnetic field and constant changes, it is impossible to predict what the field will be like in the future.
Depending on the geographical latitude, the strength of the earth's magnetic field varies. It is strongest around the poles and weakest in Ecuador.
The field strength on the Earth's surface ranges from less than 30 microtesla (0.3 Gauss) in an area that includes most of South America and South Africa to over 60 microtesla (0.6 Gauss) around the magnetic poles in northern Canada and southern Australia, and in part of Siberia.
In our area, a magnetic field of half a gauss passes through each of us.
